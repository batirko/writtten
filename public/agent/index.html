Connect your agent

Review your draft with a coding agent you already run. No API key, and the document never leaves your machine.

Normally writtten holds a model key and calls a provider from your browser. If you already run a coding agent — Claude Code, Codex, or another — it can do the reading instead. It uses the model access you already pay for, and your document travels no further than a process on your own computer.

How it works

  1. writtten mints a prompt. “Connect your agent” in Settings generates a one-time token and a short list of candidate ports, baked into a copyable prompt.
  2. You paste it into your agent, once. It carries the whole instruction set: your agent writes a small zero-dependency bridge script to disk and runs it on 127.0.0.1. Nothing is ever carried back by hand.
  3. writtten connects out to that bridge and pushes a snapshot every time your text settles. Nothing is sent while you are still typing.
  4. Your agent reviews and submits. Accepted observations appear in your feed labelled with your agent’s name, so you always know which critic is speaking.

What it cannot do

writtten’s premise is that you write every word, and a connected agent gets no exception. The bridge accepts observations and nothing else: no edit endpoint, no suggestion endpoint, no comment thread, no way to appear in the document as a co-author. That is not a permission you could grant it — those messages do not exist in the protocol, so there is nothing to ask for.

What comes back is checked

An external agent is treated as untrusted input, like any network peer. Every submission passes through a boundary before it can reach your feed:

  • It must name one of the nine observation types writtten already uses.
  • It must be phrased as an observation, not a fix. Prescriptive wording, leading questions, and hedges are rejected by the same rules writtten holds its own model to.
  • It anchors by quoting your text verbatim, and never sees writtten’s internal identifiers. A quote that doesn’t resolve is rejected, not quietly downgraded.
  • Anything you have dismissed stays dismissed, and no source can flood the feed past a fixed budget.

What the boundary can’t check is whether the observation is any good. writtten’s own checks sit behind precision floors and a fixture suite that fails the build when quality regresses. A connected agent’s do not, and cannot. That is why every external card is labelled — so a source that wastes your attention is one you can learn to discount, rather than a feed you stop trusting.

Where your document goes

With a connected agent, writtten sends your document nowhere. It travels over loopback to a process on your own machine, and from there only wherever your agent already sends its context, under terms you already accepted. That is a stronger claim than the key-based path can make — and the edge worth naming is that once your text reaches your agent, writtten has no visibility into it and no control over it. The privacy page says the same at more length.

The document is data, not instructions. Text you paste from somewhere you don’t control could try to instruct your agent, the way it could in any tool that hands a document to an agent. The skill tells your agent to treat document content as material to review and never as instructions — but that is advice, and what your agent does inside its own environment is governed by your agent’s permission model, not by writtten. Whatever comes back is validated either way, so the worst case is a rejected submission, not an edited document.

Browser support

The connection is a web page talking to a server on your own machine, and browsers differ on whether they allow that.

  • Chrome and Edge work. Recent versions ask once for permission to reach your local network — allow it, or the bridge can’t answer.
  • Firefox works, with no prompt.
  • Safari does not, and has no setting to change it. Use one of the others, or run writtten locally.

You also need a terminal to run the bridge in, which makes this a desktop feature. On a phone, add a key instead.

The skill

The prompt writtten hands your agent comes from one canonical file, which also contains the bridge script. You are welcome to read it first — it is in the repository, and the script it embeds is the exact one the tests spawn, so what is published and what runs cannot drift apart.
