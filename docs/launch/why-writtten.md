# Why I built writtten

_A short launch essay. The pitch is the philosophy; this is the version to adapt for Show HN, a blog post, or a README long-form._

---

Every AI writing tool has the same shape: the machine produces text, and you edit it. Ask, generate, accept. It's fast, and it's frictionless, and that's exactly the problem.

Because the part it makes frictionless is the thinking.

In knowledge work, the document isn't the point — it's the residue. The point is the thinking that produced it: the argument you had to make coherent, the contradiction you had to resolve, the claim you had to actually stand behind. When you offload the writing to a model, you offload that too. You get a clean draft and a slightly emptier head. Do it for a year and you've traded your judgment for convenience without noticing the exchange rate.

**writtten inverts it.** You write every word. The AI never touches your prose. Instead it rides alongside — a live feed of _observations_ that react to what you've written:

> _"This section contradicts the success metric you set in §2."_

And then it stops. It doesn't offer the fix. There is no "Apply suggestion" button, and there never will be. That single restraint is the whole product. The moment we hand you the corrected sentence, we've put you back in the passenger seat — and we've built Grammarly with a philosophy slide.

Withholding the fix turns out to be the honest move _and_ the defensible one. An observation is checkable — a contradiction either exists or it doesn't. A rewrite is taste. So the AI does the part it can be trusted with (noticing) and leaves you the part that's yours (deciding).

It's also quiet when it should be. The dangerous offloading happens during idea formation, so while you're drafting — half-formed sentences, a rough paragraph — it says nothing. Silence during thinking is a feature. It warms up only as your text settles and gets pointed during revision, when scrutiny sharpens instead of substitutes.

The hero capability is **internal contradiction detection**: it caught something you wrote that conflicts with something else you wrote, and you went and fixed it yourself. Grammarly can't do that. A chatbot won't do it unless you ask. It's a small "whoa" that leaves your ownership completely intact.

It's local-first — your document and its analysis stay in your browser, no account required — and open source, because an idea like this deserves scrutiny and collaborators more than it deserves a moat.

I built it for the way I actually want to work: not with something that thinks _for_ me, but with something that makes me think harder. If that sounds like you, [try it](../../README.md) — and tell me where it's wrong. Especially where it's wrong; the false positives are the most useful thing you can send.

— _writtten is early and honest about it. See the README's status box for what works and what doesn't yet._
